How to find 1:1 Orthologs

The entry has more than one ortholog in the other organism but all orthologous entries have only one ortholog in this organism. This implies that the gene was duplicated in an ancestor of the other organism, but after the speciation event.

The entry has only one ortholog in the other organism, but the orthologous entry has more than one ortholog in this organism. This implies that the gene was duplicated in an ancestor of this organism, but after the speciation event.

The entry has more than one ortholog in the other organism and the orthologous entries have more than one ortholog in this organism. This implies that the gene was duplicated at least twice: in an ancestor of this organism and in an ancestor of the other organism, but was the same gene in their common ancestor.

In the case of many:1 and many:many orthologs, entries that are orthologous to at least one of the orthologs of this entry.

Hierarchical Groups Hierarchical groups contain genes that descend from a single common ancestor within a given taxonomic range.

OMA groups contain a number of entries of which every entry is orthologous to every other entry in the group. This implies that there is at most one entry from each organism in a group.
